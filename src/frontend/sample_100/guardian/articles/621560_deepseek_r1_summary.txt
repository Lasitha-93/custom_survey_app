West Bromwich Albion manager Tony Pulis publicly accepted responsibility for his team's 3-0 home defeat to Manchester City, acknowledging his tactical error in abandoning defensive solidity for attacking ambition. In Monday's Premier League opener, Pulis unexpectedly started two strikers – Saido Berahino and Rickie Lambert – despite his reputation for caution. This left midfield exposed, allowing David Silva to dominate and Yaya Touré to seal the game with a 24th-minute strike. Pulis admitted post-match: "I take responsibility for the way we set up... If I had my time again I’d probably do it differently." He cited optimism from May's win over Chelsea and wanting to "be positive" in the first home game as misguided motivations. The manager noted his pivotal mistake was neglecting his usual midfield reinforcement against elite opposition. This rare public accountability contrasts sharply with common managerial deflection tactics. While José Mourinho recently blamed Chelsea medical staff for dropped points, and Sam Allardyce criticized players and officials after West Ham's collapse against Tottenham, Pulis offered no excuses. He previously accepted blame for West Brom's 4-1 loss at Arsenal last season. Analysts suggest such honesty demonstrates strength rather than weakness, fostering dressing-room respect through self-reflection. Despite fears that admitting flaws undermines authority, Pulis's track record provides confidence. His players are expected to respond positively, knowing future setups will likely revert to pragmatic foundations against top teams. The incident highlights football's broader reluctance toward accountability, where managers often target referees or invent distractions. Pulis's approach – likened to elite players owning high-profile mistakes – sets a precedent for maturity in a profession rife with evasion. His willingness to "take the bullet" may encourage similar candor across the league.
