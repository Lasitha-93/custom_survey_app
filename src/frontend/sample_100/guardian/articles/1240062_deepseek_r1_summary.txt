Guardian readers exchanged diverse literary recommendations and critiques last week, highlighting books for film adaptation and recent reads. User fat_hamster proposed Emma Donoghue’s "Room" for cinema, while Dylanwolf suggested Ann Harries’ "Manly Pursuits," set against the Boer War. Jantar advocated for Neil Gaiman’s "Graveyard Book" directed in Peter Greenaway’s avant-garde style. Recent reading included AbdullahM’s "Batman: The Black Mirror" and WWI rugby history "The Final Whistle." Dylanwolf completed Kenzaburō Ōe’s "Silent Cry" and Georges Perec’s "Life: A User’s Manual." JudgeDAmNation read Patrick O’Brien’s naval epic "Post Captain" and Lee Child’s thriller "Without Fail." John Le Carré’s works sparked debate, with AlleinAllein praising "A Perfect Spy" as his most ambitious and structurally complex novel, calling it a "thrilling page turner" despite its difficulty. Sara Richards noted the "devastating" conclusion of "A Delicate Truth," questioning state accountability. Anniversary tributes included saadskhan revisiting Shakespeare’s "Antony and Cleopatra" for the Bard’s 450th birthday, and ID980815 researching Laurie Lee’s centenary. Csrees97 recommended Gita Mehta’s "A River Sutra" as universally uplifting. Jantar broke form to critique Isabel Allende’s "Ripper" as underwhelming and overlong, advising readers to avoid it. MsCarey paused Cormac McCarthy’s Border Trilogy after "All the Pretty Horses," switching to Hari Kunzru’s "The Impressionist." Readers shared tales of frequently stolen books: Trevor Edward Walder lost copies of war novel "The Last Blue Sea" and intelligence history "Very Special Intelligence." SnowyJohn humorously lamented failed gifts of Cormac McCarthy’s "Blood Meridian" and Anthony Burgess’s works. The blog invites further contributions via GuardianWitness for next week’s edition.
