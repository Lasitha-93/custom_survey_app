**UK Prepares for Air Strikes Against ISIS in Iraq** Prime Minister David Cameron will urge MPs to approve UK participation in coalition air strikes against Islamic State (ISIS) militants in Iraq during an emergency parliamentary debate on Friday. He convened his cabinet Thursday to finalize plans, emphasizing that ISIS poses a "clear and present danger" to Britain and that action has a legal basis following a formal request from Iraq's government. Cameron secured conditional support from Labour and Liberal Democrat leaders, limiting initial operations to Iraq. Any extension to Syria would require a separate parliamentary vote. The Prime Minister warned against inaction driven by fears of repeating past Iraq war errors, describing ISIS as "psychopathic, murderous, brutal" extremists whose medieval-style atrocities—including beheadings and rape—demand a global response. Addressing the UN General Assembly, Cameron stressed military action must be part of a "comprehensive, intelligent" strategy involving regional partners, potentially including Iran. He ruled out deploying British ground combat troops but confirmed support for training local forces like Iraq's Peshmerga. Defence costs remain unquantified due to uncertain mission duration. Lib Dem leader Nick Clegg backed the move, citing a "moral duty" to assist Iraq, while Labour's Ed Miliband insisted Syrian operations remain off-limits without further authorization. US President Barack Obama reinforced the coalition's resolve at the UN, declaring ISIS "understands only the language of force." Cameron acknowledged risks to hostages but asserted the UK must proceed with a multi-pronged approach combining humanitarian, diplomatic, and military efforts.
