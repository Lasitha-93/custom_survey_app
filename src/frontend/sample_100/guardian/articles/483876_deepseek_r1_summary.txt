Modern technology enables unprecedented surveillance that could abolish privacy, allowing authorities to solve crimes by accessing comprehensive digital records from phones, computers, IoT devices, health monitors, and CCTV. While proponents like Home Secretary Theresa May see this as ideal, critics warn such centralized power invites state tyranny and criminal data leaks. Current safeguards include decentralized data storage—much information, like email content scanned for ads, remains unread—and legal frameworks. However, UK surveillance laws are outdated. May’s proposed replacement faces sharp criticism from three parliamentary committees as "uncosted, unjustified, and unworkable." Key objections focus on two aspects: First, the mandate for telecoms and ISPs to retain all connection records for a year, including public Wi-Fi networks. Experts question its technical feasibility, predicting costs far exceeding the Home Office’s £150m estimate—potentially tenfold before failure. Second, May’s claim of "technological neutrality" is deemed impossible given privacy’s evolving legal and social nature versus rapid tech advancements. Lawmakers argue the 300-page draft bill lacks clear principles and realistic costing, demanding a rewrite and provisions for review within five years to address emerging threats.
