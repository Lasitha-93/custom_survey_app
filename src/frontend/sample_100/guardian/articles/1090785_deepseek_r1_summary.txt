**News of the World Phone-Hacking Scandal Widens Amid Executive Denials** Key figures at Rupert Murdoch's News of the World have repeatedly denied widespread knowledge of illegal phone-hacking by staff, despite mounting evidence contradicting their claims. The scandal erupted in 2007 when royal editor Clive Goodman and private investigator Glenn Mulcaire were jailed for intercepting voicemails of palace aides and public figures including MP Simon Hughes, model Elle Macpherson, and PR executive Max Clifford. Executives insisted Goodman acted alone. Then-editor Andy Coulson resigned but denied awareness; his successor Colin Myler called it a "rogue exception." Former chairman Les Hinton and managing editor Stuart Kuttner both told inquiries only Goodman was involved. Kuttner stepped down this week amid renewed scrutiny. However, Guardian investigations reveal systematic illegal activities beyond Goodman. News Group Newspapers paid over £1 million to settle cases, including £700,000 to Professional Footballers' Association chief Gordon Taylor after messages from Alan Shearer and Alex Shearer were hacked. Confidential information was also illicitly obtained from agencies for targets like Nigella Lawson, Anne Robinson, and Lenny Henry. Alleged victims span politics and entertainment, including John Prescott, Boris Johnson, Tessa Jowell, George Michael, Gwyneth Paltrow, and Jade Goody. Prescott urged Conservative leader David Cameron—who hired Coulson as communications director—to dismiss him. The scope suggests editorial involvement during Rebekah Wade’s tenure as editor; Wade is set to become News International CEO. Murdoch’s company faces ongoing legal and parliamentary pressure as new allegations emerge.
