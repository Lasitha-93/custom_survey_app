Facebook has settled Federal Trade Commission charges of deceptive privacy practices, agreeing to 20 years of independent privacy audits. The FTC accused the social media giant of making users' confidential profile information public without consent and sharing personal data—including status updates, location, and marital status—with advertisers and third-party apps without adequate disclosure. Key violations centered on Facebook's 2010 privacy settings overhaul, which allegedly removed users' ability to restrict access to their name, profile picture, gender, friend lists, pages, and networks without proper notification. The FTC also cited instances where photos and videos remained public after account deactivation. The settlement follows a record $22.5 million FTC fine against Google for circumventing Apple Safari privacy protections, intensifying scrutiny over data security at dominant online platforms. Facebook serves over 955 million monthly active users, while Google commands two-thirds of the U.S. search market. FTC commissioners approved the settlement 3-1 with one abstention. Dissenting Commissioner J. Thomas Rosch objected to Facebook's denial of liability, advocating for a "neither confirm nor deny" stance—mirroring his position in Google's settlement. Google, operating under an existing consent order, denied liability but paid its fine.
