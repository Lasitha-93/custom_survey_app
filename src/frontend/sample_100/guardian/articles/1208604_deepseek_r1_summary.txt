**Major Holocaust Study and Literary Highlights Announced** Historian David Cesarani’s final work, *Final Solution: The Fate of the Jews 1933–1949*, argues Nazi genocide escalated erratically under wartime pressures, extending to postwar displaced persons camps. Key releases include Julian Barnes’ Stalin-era novel *The Noise of Time*, inspired by composer Shostakovich, and Anne Tyler’s *Vinegar Girl*, a modern retelling of *The Taming of the Shrew*. **Notable Non-Fiction** Lisa Randall’s *Dark Matter and the Dinosaurs* proposes cosmic connections in extinction events. Posthumous works feature Seamus Heaney’s translation of Virgil’s *Aeneid: Book VI* and Christopher Hitchens’ essay collection *And Yet*. Siddhartha Mukherjee examines genetics in *The Gene*, while Yanis Varoufakis critiques austerity in *And the Weak Suffer What They Must?*. **Fiction and Adaptations** Don DeLillo’s *Zero K* explores immortality, and Joe Hill’s *The Fireman* depicts pandemic-driven chaos. BBC adaptations include *War and Peace* (January 3) and John le Carré’s *The Night Manager*. Film releases: *Room* (January 15), *The Jungle Book* (April 15), and Spielberg’s *The BFG* (July 22). **Significant Anniversaries** 2016 marks Shakespeare’s 400th death anniversary (April 23) with commemorative events. Other milestones: Truman Capote’s *In Cold Blood* (50 years, January 10), Charlotte Brontë’s bicentenary (April 21), and James Joyce’s *A Portrait of the Artist as a Young Man* (100 years, December 29). **Award Contenders and Debuts** Man Booker winner Howard Jacobson reimagines *The Merchant of Venice* in *Shylock Is My Name*. Debuts: Nicholas Searle’s reverse-narrative thriller *The Good Liar* and Emma Cline’s Manson-inspired *The Girls*.
