The Australian government faces scrutiny over allegations it paid people smugglers $30,000 to turn back an asylum seeker boat, despite ministers previously denying such payments occurred. Officials now refuse to comment, citing national security concerns—a shift from last week when Foreign Minister Julie Bishop and Immigration Minister Peter Dutton explicitly denied cash transactions. Evidence includes testimonies from asylum seekers to Indonesian and UN officials, along with photographs of money in plastic bags. Customs personnel involved report to the government but remain silent. Critics challenge the national security justification, arguing this concerns potential bribery rather than intelligence operations. They note that denying the payments would deter people smugglers and ease tensions with Indonesia. Former minister Philip Ruddock suggested $30,000 was cheaper than onshore processing, but opponents stress cost doesn’t override legal obligations. An unnamed source claimed an Australian Secret Intelligence Service officer may have facilitated the payment. Labor and the Greens are investigating, though Labor’s credibility is undermined by lacking an alternative policy. The controversy tests whether the government’s "stop the boats" goal justifies evading accountability and potential breaches of domestic and international law.
