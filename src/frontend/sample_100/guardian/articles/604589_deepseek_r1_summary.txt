Director Quentin Tarantino dismissed concerns over threats from U.S. police unions targeting his upcoming film "The Hateful Eight," stating he feels no worry about their undisclosed plans. The controversy began in October when Tarantino declared he was "on the side of the murdered" at a New York rally against police violence. The Patrolmen’s Benevolent Association, led by Patrick Lynch, urged a boycott of his films, with other unions joining. The Fraternal Order of Police escalated tensions by warning it had "something in the works" to economically harm the film’s December release, citing "the element of surprise." At a Los Angeles press conference Saturday, Tarantino addressed the threats: "I do not feel like the police force is this sinister black-hand organization," but criticized public servants for issuing threats. He speculated unions might picket screenings, laughing off suggestions they could buy tickets to empty theaters. Tarantino acknowledged respecting police in affluent areas like Hollywood Hills but argued militarization since the "war on drugs" created mistrust in minority communities. "You should be able to talk about abuses of power... without the police organization targeting you," he said. On his artistic approach, Tarantino stated: "As an artist, I don’t think about political correctness at all," emphasizing his role is to ignore social critics regarding character portrayals. "The Hateful Eight" premieres in select 70mm theaters December 25, with a wide release January 18, 2016.
