UN Secretary-General Ban Ki-moon warned world leaders against abandoning anti-poverty commitments amid economic pressures, declaring "we should not balance budgets on the backs of the poor" at the UN summit reviewing Millennium Development Goals (MDGs). Addressing over 140 leaders in New York, Ban acknowledged progress in education, disease control, and technology access since the 2000 pledge but stressed "the clock is ticking" with key targets unmet by the 2015 deadline. He urged sustained aid as "a lifeline for billions," especially for maternal and child health—the most lagging goal. Britain committed to increasing aid to 0.7% of national income by 2013, with Development Secretary Andrew Mitchell demanding greater transparency and measurable results. France pledged a 20% boost to its Global Fund contribution for AIDS, TB, and malaria over three years, totaling €360 million. President Nicolas Sarkozy advocated a financial transaction tax to fund MDGs, noting malaria kills one African child every minute. Spain endorsed the tax proposal. IMF head Dominique Strauss-Kahn warned crises derailed progress, requiring "redoubled efforts." Outside the summit, protesters demanded increased HIV/AIDS funding, criticizing the Obama administration for unmet promises. Amnesty International activated a "maternal death clock" in Times Square, highlighting one childbirth-related death every 90 seconds—underscoring urgency as leaders review pledges to reduce poverty and inequality.
