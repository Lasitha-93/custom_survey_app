British MPs warn that youth unemployment in developing nations is a "ticking time bomb" requiring urgent global action comparable to humanitarian crises or disease eradication. The Commons International Development Committee report highlights Africa's population surge, predicting 600 million young people will compete for just 200 million jobs over the next decade—risking widespread unrest. Committee chair Sir Malcolm Bruce criticized "complacent assumptions" about slowing population growth and urged matching the passion devoted to children's vaccinations. While acknowledging UK aid agency DfID's increased economic spending (£1.8bn this year), the report cites "several concerns" about its private-sector engagement—which generates 90% of new jobs. Recommendations include hiring business-experienced advisers and broadening focus beyond agriculture/manufacturing to tourism, health, and education, where millions of jobs could emerge. The committee demanded targeted help for marginalized groups, noting women's limited opportunities due to unpaid care work. It questioned DfID's cuts to reproductive health funding amid high African fertility rates and its decision to end core funding for the International Labour Organisation (ILO), described as vital for employment expertise. Shadow development secretary Mary Creagh condemned the government’s "trickle-down approach," pledging Labour would reverse ILO cuts and boost health/education spending. DfID cited job-creation successes—including training 70,000 Nepalese youth and supporting 25,000 female entrepreneurs—and affirmed economic development as central to poverty reduction.
