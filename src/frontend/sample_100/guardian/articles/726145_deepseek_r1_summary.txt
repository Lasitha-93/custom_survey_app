Nick Clegg's reputation remains damaged by his tuition fees U-turn, and the "Cleggmania" of 2010 feels like "ancient history," according to his former strategy director, Richard Reeves. Writing in the Guardian, Reeves also predicted the 2015 general election campaign would be uninspiring, with weak economic conditions and leaders David Cameron, Ed Miliband, and Clegg all lacking freshness. Reeves accurately forecast Clegg would adopt an unapologetically pro-European stance focused on "steadiness and consistency." This was immediately confirmed by Clegg's New Year message, where he pledged "more steady as she goes" and attacked rivals over Europe. Clegg accused the Conservatives of "flirting with exit" from the EU, jeopardizing Britain's economic recovery, while claiming Labour lacked courage and UKIP wanted out. He positioned the Liberal Democrats as "Britain's party of in" due to the economic benefits of membership. Clegg urged pro-European voters to support the Liberal Democrats in the May European elections to secure Britain's EU future, rather than waiting for the general election. Reeves suggested this pro-European vote could be crucial for the party. Despite facing the "steepest climb" to succeed in 2015, Reeves argued the Liberal Democrats might still deny Cameron or Miliband victory by concentrating resources in key seats. He added the party could target centre-right voters, as it was no longer mistaken for an adjunct of the left. Reeves ruled out any leadership challenge to Clegg but foresaw a lacklustre campaign overall.
