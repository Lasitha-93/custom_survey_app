James Desborough, a former award-winning reporter for the defunct News of the World, has been arrested by detectives investigating the phone-hacking scandal. He presented himself at a south London police station and was arrested on suspicion of conspiring to intercept communications. Desborough, the 13th person arrested under Operation Weeting, is significant as the most junior journalist detained so far. The allegations relate to activities before his promotion to US editor in April 2009, shortly after winning a British Press Award. His subsequent move to Los Angeles raises questions about whether hacking techniques were employed in the US and if others there were involved. His arrest directly challenges News International's (NI) long-standing claim that phone-hacking at the tabloid was an isolated issue ending in 2007 with the jailing of royal correspondent Clive Goodman and private investigator Glenn Mulcaire. Mulcaire, central to the scandal, is now suing NI via a high court writ after the company, part of Rupert Murdoch's News Corp, stopped paying his legal bills in July. Separately, a source close to US investigations indicated American authorities would launch their own inquiry if evidence emerged Desborough engaged in illegal hacking while based there. Desborough worked for the News of the World from 2005 until its closure last month, known for breaking major celebrity stories.
