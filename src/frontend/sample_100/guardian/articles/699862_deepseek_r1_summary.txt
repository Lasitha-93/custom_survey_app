A British commentator has decried the displacement of traditional biscuits by oversized American-style cookies and baked goods in coffee chains, arguing they represent a decline in quality and cultural identity. The writer criticizes establishments for promoting "cookies the size of hubcaps" and other items like granola bars, cupcakes, and whoopie pies, describing them as over-decorated, bland, and poor value. American coffee chains are blamed for altering UK tea and coffee culture—citing practices like charging extra for larger teas and confusing terminology—while their baked offerings are dismissed as "pumped-up, showboating, faux-bakery items." The author contrasts these with "quiet, restrained" British classics like digestives, gingernuts, and Bourbons, which offer "private pleasure" without excess. While acknowledging a place for indulgent treats, the writer asserts commercial versions prioritize style over substance, calling them a "total rip-off." Instead, they advocate homemade baking with quality ingredients, providing four traditional biscuit recipes as alternatives: Jam Thumbprint, Peanut Butter Chocolate Sandwich, Shrewsbury, and Cornish Fairings. These recipes emphasize simplicity and authenticity over industrial-scale production. The piece frames the shift as a cultural loss, urging a return to modest, time-honored British biscuits.
