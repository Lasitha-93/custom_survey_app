The murder of Özgecan Aslan in Turkey ignited nationwide outrage, spurring unprecedented discussions on sexual violence and femicide. Thousands of women shared personal harassment experiences via the hashtag #sendeanlat, reflecting a widespread urge to expose systemic failures. Feminist groups have long highlighted "kadın cinayetleri" (femicide), with organizations like Bianet documenting male violence since 2009. A digital monument tracks women murdered since 2012, though official records remain absent. Turkey’s media and pop culture historically normalized violence against women, from Yeşilçam films to sensationalized news reports that often blamed victims. Aslan’s case diverged: killed by a stranger during an alleged rape attempt, she was unambiguously portrayed as innocent, avoiding typical media justifications like domestic disputes. This shifted public reaction, with many condemning exploitative coverage. In response, women increasingly advocate self-defense, including carrying knives. The Arin Mirxan Women’s Defence Task Force in Diyarbakir declared women’s right to violent self-protection, while social media fills with accounts of resisting harassers. However, state conservatism persists. President Erdoğan recently reinforced traditional gender roles, dismissing feminists who reject male "custodianship" of women. Despite rising awareness, systemic flaws endure—inadequate justice systems deter reporting, and media reforms lag. Women’s groups demand legal changes and societal mindset shifts, emphasizing Aslan’s death as a catalyst for unhesitating public outcry. The challenge remains translating this momentum into action.
