Nigeria faces critical immunization gaps despite having one of the world's largest child populations, with fewer than half of infants receiving basic vaccines like DTP (diphtheria, tetanus, pertussis). Alarmingly, DTP coverage has declined to 47%, even as global immunization efforts intensify. Nigeria remains one of only three polio-endemic countries, recording 108 polio cases last year. Dr. David Olayemi of Save the Children attributes this crisis partly to unintended consequences of polio eradication campaigns. Nigeria's weak primary healthcare system is further strained as health workers—lured by incentive payments from organizations like Rotary and the Gates Foundation—desert clinics for monthly polio drives. This leaves routine immunizations neglected. Additionally, "vaccination fatigue" has emerged: repeated polio doses for children erode family trust, leading to refusals. Mothers' immunization cards are sometimes lost or rejected by workers paid per child reached. The solution, emphasized at the Gavi Partners Forum, involves integrating polio vaccination into routine immunization and strengthening primary healthcare. Gavi Alliance has committed to this approach. A Save the Children report urges targeted strategies to reach the poorest 20% of unvaccinated children globally, advocating outreach clinics and robust health systems. Experts stress that routine immunization must become normalized beyond emergency polio responses.
