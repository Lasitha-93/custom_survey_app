Moody's downgraded Spain's sovereign credit rating to Aa2 on Thursday, escalating eurozone debt concerns by warning the banking sector rescue could cost €40-50 billion—more than double its prior €17 billion estimate. The agency cited "meaningful risk" that Spain underestimated recapitalization expenses despite ongoing restructuring of savings banks (cajas). The downgrade intensified market pressure: the euro fell to a one-week low against the dollar, European stocks declined, and borrowing costs surged for vulnerable eurozone nations. Credit default swaps for Spain, Greece, and Portugal widened significantly, reflecting heightened default fears. Spain's government swiftly criticized the timing, noting updated banking reform plans—including partial nationalization and public listings of cajas—would be released Thursday evening. Treasury director Soledad Núñez asserted Moody's overlooked Spain's deficit-cutting efforts and pension reforms. Analysts warned the move strains Europe's €440 billion rescue fund (EFSF), which Rabobank's Jane Foley said could be "almost wiped out" if Spain and Portugal require bailouts. She urged bolstering the EFSF to reassure markets ahead of Friday's critical EU summit. Markit's Gavan Nolan noted rating agencies are reasserting market influence after Greece's multi-notch downgrade earlier this week, compounding uncertainty as leaders confront sovereign debt contagion.
