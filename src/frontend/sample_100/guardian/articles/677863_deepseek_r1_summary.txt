Newspapers worldwide are intensifying paywall strategies amid industry turmoil, with Winnipeg Free Press pioneering a micropayment model charging C$0.27 per article alongside its C$16.99 monthly subscription. This "supermarket basket" approach contrasts with dominant models: blanket subscriptions (Murdoch outlets), ad-funded free access (Mail Online, Guardian), and metered access allowing limited free articles (FT, Telegraph, Economist – adopted by Northern Echo and Washington Post). A notable innovation emerges from The Browser, a UK-based curation service founded by Robert Cottrell. Its "1Pass" system enables users to deposit funds ($20/year subscription plus top-ups) to purchase individual articles behind paywalls at partner sites like the Financial Times, Economist, New York Review of Books, and Foreign Affairs. This aggregates access across multiple premium publishers, bypassing individual subscription commitments and free-article limits. Publishers retain control, avoiding platform dominance by tech giants. While digital newspaper revenue is projected to reach $19bn within four years, micropayments face uncertainty. Challenges include consumer resistance to upfront fees for single articles and the viability of small charges. Over 700 US papers have implemented paywalls, but The Browser's model offers a potential flexible solution for readers seeking specific content without full subscriptions. Its expansion potential includes broader paywall roaming, though industry adoption remains unproven.
