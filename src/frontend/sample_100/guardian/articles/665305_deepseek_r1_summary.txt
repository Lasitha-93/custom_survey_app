David Lynch's intimate drawings on everyday ephemera like Post-it notes, napkins, and matchboxes are compiled in a new book published by Steidl and Fondation Cartier. These small-scale works, created spontaneously during film productions including "Blue Velvet" and "Dune," reveal the director's surrealist visual artistry beyond cinema. The collection features abstract forms, dreamlike landscapes with doppelganger trees, and enigmatic creatures—some reminiscent of Rothko's color fields or Dalí's vistas. Lynch employed meticulous shading techniques on found materials, transforming casual doodles into cohesive artistic expressions. Highlights include matchbox mini-suites, whirlwind scribbles from his workspace, and a horse-like contraption with ambiguous mechanical elements. This publication showcases Lynch's lesser-known graphic practice, capturing fleeting ideas during his creative process. The works range from twisted musical notations to a humorous "Brunsing pig" sketch, demonstrating his distinctive blend of spontaneity and technical skill.
