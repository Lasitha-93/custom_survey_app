**Gehry Retrospective Spotlights Career and Controversial River Plan** Frank Gehry, 86, remains a defining force in global architecture despite recent back surgery, with a Los Angeles County Museum of Art retrospective showcasing his six-decade career through March 20. The exhibition features 66 models and 200 drawings, highlighting iconic works like L.A.’s Disney Concert Hall—noted for its tactile titanium walls—and his 1977 Santa Monica home renovation, which used chainlink fencing and corrugated metal to challenge conventional aesthetics. Gehry’s unorthodox approach, cultivated in L.A.’s artist circles away from New York’s factions, evolved dramatically after his 1989 Pritzker Prize win. Adoption of aerospace software CATIA enabled fluid designs like Bilbao’s Guggenheim Museum and Prague’s "Dancing House." Current projects include Facebook’s campus, Philadelphia Museum renovations, and West Hollywood’s 8150 Sunset towers. Controversy surrounds Gehry’s pro bono L.A. River revitalization plan, developed secretly with city affiliates. Recently revealed details propose a "linear Central Park" but omit specifics for much of the 52-mile waterway, drawing ire from community groups excluded from consultations. Gehry aims to cover downtown railroad tracks, creating a park inspired by New York’s High Line, declaring it could "make the High Line look like a little pishy thing." Despite a $1 billion federal grant, the plan’s future remains uncertain pending public release. Gehry emphasized architecture’s role in creating "uplifting" spaces that "make life pleasanter," though his river project’s scope and community engagement remain unresolved. *(Word count: 249)*
