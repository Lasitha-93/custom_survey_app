Pressure is mounting on Director of Public Prosecutions Alison Saunders to resign after her decision not to prosecute Lord Janner over historical sexual abuse allegations was overturned. An independent QC review has directed that Janner, 86, face a "trial of the facts," the Crown Prosecution Service will announce Monday. This marks the first courtroom airing of claims against the former Labour peer, investigated unsuccessfully three times since the 1960s. Saunders ruled in April that prosecution was not in the public interest due to Janner’s dementia, which renders him unfit to plead. Alleged victims challenged this under the "right to review" process—a rare successful overturning of a DPP decision. Saunders had overruled specialist child abuse QC Eleanor Laws, who recommended charges. Leicestershire Police, which spent two years investigating, called Saunders' decision "perverse" and threatened legal action. Alleged victims received hand-delivered letters confirming the reversal. One anonymous victim stated Saunders "should go" for delaying justice, while Paul Miller, who claims Janner abused him at age nine, called her "incompetent" and demanded her sacking. MPs intensified pressure: Labour’s Simon Danczuk cited Saunders’ "major mistakes," and Conservative David Davis questioned her judgment, though he stopped short of seeking resignation. Labour’s John Mann opposed resignation, stressing the need for resources to investigate historical abuse claims. The trial of facts will let a jury determine if Janner committed alleged acts, but no guilty verdict or sentence is possible due to his condition. Lawyers for victims welcomed the decision as restoring faith in justice after decades of denial. Janner’s family maintains his innocence.
