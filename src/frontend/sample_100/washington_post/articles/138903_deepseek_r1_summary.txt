NASA postponed the inaugural test flight of its Orion spacecraft Thursday due to malfunctioning valves, delaying a critical step toward eventual human missions to Mars. The uncrewed launch, already hampered by strong winds and an unauthorized boat near Cape Canaveral, Florida, was scrubbed after fuel and drain valves failed to close properly. United Launch Alliance (ULA), builder of the Delta IV Heavy rocket carrying Orion, attributed the valve issue to cold temperatures making them "sluggish." The next launch attempt is scheduled for Friday at 7:05 a.m. EST, though officials cite only a 40% chance of favorable weather. This test flight aims to send Orion 3,600 miles from Earth—15 times farther than the International Space Station and beyond any human-rated spacecraft since Apollo 17 in 1972. Key objectives include testing the heat shield against 4,000-degree reentry temperatures, radiation tolerance in deep space, parachute deployment, and separation systems. NASA Administrator Charles Bolden emphasized Orion’s role in advancing Mars exploration, calling it a revival of Apollo-era ambitions after a "40-year hiatus." Originally part of the canceled Constellation moon program, Orion now targets Mars under Obama administration goals. While this flight uses ULA’s rocket, future missions will rely on NASA’s developing Space Launch System. A crewed Orion mission remains at least seven years away.
