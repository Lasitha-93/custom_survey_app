**African American Women Challenge Marriage Pressures, Critique Dating Advice** By Nicole Moliere African American women face intense societal pressure to marry, often framed as essential to their worth, argues writer Nicole Moliere. In a critique of pervasive relationship advice—specifically targeting Steve Harvey’s "Act Like a Lady, Think Like a Man"—Moliere contends such narratives reduce women to stereotypes like "gold diggers" or "old maids" while exploiting fears of loneliness. Harvey’s book, she writes, promotes a "fear-based" message implying Black men cannot offer faithful commitment and Black women will accept destructive behavior to avoid being alone. This dynamic, Moliere asserts, insults both genders and ignores women’s capacity for deep, self-determined connections. Marriage itself is not inherently superior to singlehood, Moliere emphasizes. Traditional marriage often demands sacrificing personal identity and ambitions, particularly for women. Citing Eleanore Wells of TheSpinsterliciousLife.com, she notes marriage is "not a better life—it’s a different one." Many women lose themselves in marital roles, facing unfulfilled expectations akin to "desiring grilled salmon but getting McDonald’s." Instead, Moliere advocates for self-love and rejecting external validation. African American women, she argues, should embrace their full identities without seeking approval through relationships. Prioritizing personal growth and rejecting "antiquated advice" allows women to define fulfilling partnerships on their own terms—including with themselves. Moliere, creator of the dating blog SexyBrainiac.com, shares her own fulfillment post-divorce: "I was never lonelier than when I married." She challenges readers to focus on building self-worth rather than marital status, concluding: "I’d rather be single and live my truth than married and live someone else’s lie." *(Word count: 249)*
