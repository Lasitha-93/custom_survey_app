KOCHI, India — Vigilante groups in Kerala are systematically killing stray dogs amid rising public panic over attacks, despite Supreme Court condemnation and orders to implement sterilization. The southern Indian state, home to an estimated 250,000 strays, recorded over 100,000 dog bites last year, fueling violent mob actions including public beatings and parades of carcasses. India has approximately 30 million stray dogs, with rabies causing 20,000 human deaths annually. While Kerala reported fewer than a dozen rabies deaths, bite incidents surged 14% year-over-year. Anti-dog activist Jose Maveli, who funds catchers killing hundreds annually, argues strays threaten safety and tourism: "Look at Western countries—are there dogs roaming freely?" The Supreme Court this month called widespread killings "unacceptable," mandating sterilization instead. Animal rights groups contend the crisis is exaggerated. "Politicians, media, and vigilantes have created panic mode," said activist Latha Indira. Rescuers face violence; Aishwarya Prem was assaulted by a mob in December. Kerala's sterilization programs remain underfunded at $11 per dog, far below requirements. Experts cite poor garbage management as a key cause for strays. The killing drive intensified after an elderly woman died in a dog attack two months ago. Residents like Bismi, whose 7-year-old son suffered facial bites, demand action, but veterinarians stress science over vigilante solutions. "Killing daily won’t achieve zero," said government vet Kishore Janardhanan. "Sterilization is the only answer—but panic discredits it."
