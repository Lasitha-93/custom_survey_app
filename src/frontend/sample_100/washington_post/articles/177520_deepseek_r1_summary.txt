Once symbols of suburban prosperity, American malls are undergoing a dramatic transformation as many face decline. Experts predict half of the nation's 1,500 malls built between the 1950s and 2005 may close or be reconfigured, with Washington-area examples illustrating the trend. The region's mall era peaked with openings like Landmark Mall in 1965, which drew 30,000 celebrants. However, shifting demographics and retail preferences toward urban centers have left numerous suburban malls struggling. Landmark Mall, now 40% vacant and surrounded by unwalkable infrastructure, epitomizes the challenge. Owner Howard Hughes Corp. plans to replace it with a 51-acre open-air neighborhood featuring apartments, restaurants, and tree-lined streets, targeting baby boomers returning to urban lifestyles. Similar redevelopments are underway. Springfield Mall reopened as Springfield Town Center after a $250 million renovation, adding outdoor dining and residences. Laurel Mall became Town Centre at Laurel, now 80% leased with popular retailers avoiding enclosed spaces. Tysons Corner Center, still thriving, is expanding with a $500 million project including apartments and offices connected to Metro. These conversions face hurdles, notably from anchor stores holding legal veto power. White Flint Mall's redevelopment into a massive town center stalled when Lord & Taylor sued, citing a 38-year-old agreement granting it control over design changes. The mall now stands nearly empty, highlighting disputes common nationwide where anchors demand concessions or termination fees. Developers emphasize creating walkable, authentic environments to attract shoppers and residents. Fairfax County approved adding thousands of residential units near Springfield, while Alexandria and Montgomery County projects leverage affluent demographics. Retailers increasingly prefer street-facing locations over traditional mall anchors, reducing reliance on department stores. The shift reflects broader consumer demand for mixed-use, accessible spaces. As Don Wood of Federal Realty Investment Trust noted, only about 100 dominant malls will survive, leaving others to adapt or perish. No new enclosed malls are being built, signaling the end of an era.
