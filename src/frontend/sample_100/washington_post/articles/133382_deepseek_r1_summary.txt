FIFA has charged Uruguayan soccer star Luis Suarez with biting Italy defender Giorgio Chiellini during a World Cup match, risking a ban of up to 24 months. This incident marks Suarez's third alleged biting offense, potentially sidelining Uruguay's top offensive player after his decisive goals against England. Suarez did not deny the bite, calling it one of "things that happen on the pitch." Sports psychologists attribute such acts to overwhelming emotion overpowering reason during high-stress competition. "Emotions drive athlete behavior much more than rational thought," said Boston University's Adam Naylor, noting frustration often triggers aggression. Ironically, attempts to control impulses may heighten outburst risks. Similar incidents include Mike Tyson biting Evander Holyfield's ear in 1997—an act Tyson later said he'd repeat—and NBA player Tree Rollins biting an opponent's finger to the tendon in 1983. Bites typically occur impulsively in obscured moments, like scuffles or celebrations. In 2001, Sevilla's Francisco Gallardo bit a teammate's genitals mid-celebration, drawing suspension for violating "sporting dignity." Suarez previously expressed regret for his behavior, stating, "It’s not the most attractive image." FIFA’s ruling will determine his World Cup fate. (Correction: A quote about a biting incident was misattributed to Gallardo; it was Peter Filandia who called it "a split second decision.")
