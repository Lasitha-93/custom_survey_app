President Obama delivered his final State of the Union address Tuesday, framing America’s challenges around "four big questions" while calling for "a better politics" grounded in rational debate and trust. Departing from tradition, the speech offered few specific policy proposals, instead defending his legacy and urging confidence amid rapid change. Obama challenged Americans to reject fear-driven politics—a veiled critique of Republican frontrunner Donald Trump—noting historical parallels where "those who told us to fear the future" were overcome. He dismissed claims of economic decline as "fiction" and countered foreign policy critics who portray ISIS as an existential threat, arguing such rhetoric aids terrorist recruitment. He rebuked "tough talk" like Sen. Ted Cruz’s call to "make sand glow," stating it fails on the global stage. The president outlined four overarching themes: ensuring economic opportunity amid disruption; harnessing technology for challenges like climate change and cancer (announcing a "moonshot" initiative led by Vice President Biden); maintaining security without becoming "the world’s policeman"; and reforming politics through redistricting changes, campaign finance limits, and voting rights protections. He urged lifting the Cuba embargo, declaring "the Cold War is over." Obama acknowledged deepening political rancor, admitting regret that partisan divisions worsened during his presidency: "It’s one of the few regrets of my presidency." He emphasized that progress on issues like climate change, criminal justice reform, and Guantanamo’s closure requires grassroots pressure to overcome cynicism.
