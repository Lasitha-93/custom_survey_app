Wildfires have scorched over 9.1 million acres across the U.S. so far in 2012, marking only the third time since 1960 that burned acreage has exceeded 9 million, according to the National Interagency Fire Center. The affected area is roughly equivalent to Massachusetts and Connecticut combined. While unlikely to surpass 2006’s record 9.8 million acres, the season has proven deadly, claiming 13 lives—exceeding 2011’s toll but below the 10-year average of 18. Colorado and Oregon endured historic blazes, including Colorado’s most destructive wildfires and Oregon’s largest-ever fire. August saw the most severe activity nationally, with over 3.6 million acres burned—the highest monthly total since 2000. Overall, wildfires destroyed 2,125 homes, below the annual average of 2,600, though Colorado Springs’ Waldo Canyon fire alone razed 346 residences. Economic losses exceeded $1 billion, led by the Waldo Canyon fire’s $500 million impact. Officials attribute the intense season to prolonged drought, high temperatures, and expanding development in fire-prone areas. Climate change may exacerbate future risks: a Climate Central report links rising temperatures and reduced snowpack to increased Western wildfires, noting that burned areas could quadruple per degree Celsius of warming. Despite the devastation, fire managers note that cleared lands can act as future firebreaks.
