Records obtained by USA TODAY reveal hundreds of safety incidents in U.S. laboratories handling dangerous pathogens, exposing recurring equipment failures and procedural lapses. Key examples include: At the USDA's Southeast Poultry Research Lab in Georgia, two 2014 incidents involved respirator hose failures during H5N1 avian flu experiments. Workers received antivirals but avoided infection. Lab officials cited low risk due to secondary containment measures and implemented retraining and equipment upgrades. The University of North Carolina-Chapel Hill reported eight mouse escapes from April 2013 to September 2014, some involving SARS or H1N1-infected rodents. Though the university noted a 0.001% escape rate during 54,000 handlings, the NIH deemed it "concerning" and mandated corrective actions. A Texas A&M researcher accidentally stuck himself with a needle and was bitten by a Lyme disease-infected mouse within a week in February 2014. He underwent medical prophylaxis. The university declined further comment. At Fort Detrick’s BSL-4 lab in Maryland, 37 spacesuit breaches occurred over 20 months (2013-2014) during Ebola research. Tears included visor holes and foot damage, attributed to aging equipment. The lab asserted positive air pressure prevented exposure and replaces suits regularly. A University of Wisconsin researcher punctured skin with an H5N1-contaminated needle in 2013, prompting a seven-day home quarantine. No infection resulted, but NIH criticized protocol violations. Other incidents include: Unapproved MERS virus research in a lower-safety lab at the University of Iowa (researcher probationed); leptospira exposure risks at a USDA Iowa lab due to unsafe practices; and a lost mosquito during a Colorado State dengue experiment, though testing showed no transmission. Many facilities reported no infections and conducted retraining, but NIH repeatedly intervened to demand improved safety protocols.
