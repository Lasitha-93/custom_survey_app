Dick Meredith, 82, died Sept. 1 at Iowa’s Clarinda Treatment Community after staff gave him a peanut-butter sandwich despite his doctor-ordered pureed diet due to swallowing difficulties, causing fatal choking. State inspectors fined the facility $8,250, citing staff negligence in meal labeling and protocol failures. Meredith’s family initially believed administrators’ claim that he died of a heart attack after a minor choking incident. They praised the facility in his obituary. They learned the truth months later from a Nov. 23 Des Moines Register report detailing the inspection findings. The death echoes Meredith’s traumatic history at the same institution (then Clarinda Mental Health Institute), where in 1955 he received a lobotomy without parental consent. The surgery left him permanently disabled, rendering him unable to live independently. His sister, Cleojean Olson, called the state’s handling of his death “deja vu” of past institutional failures. The Iowa Department of Human Services, which runs the facility, called the incident “isolated” and stated it took “personnel action” and reviewed dietary services. However, it declined to publicly discuss Meredith’s case, citing confidentiality. Olson, Meredith’s legal guardian, disputes this rationale and is exploring a lawsuit, seeking accountability rather than compensation. She accused the state of “dancing around” the truth.
