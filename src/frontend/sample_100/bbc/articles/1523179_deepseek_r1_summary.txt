The Met Office has downgraded heatwave warnings across most of England, lowering alerts from level three to level two ("alert and readiness") in the North West, West Midlands, and South West. Only the North East retains the lowest warning level. Despite the downgrade, temperatures are forecast to rise again next week, potentially reaching the mid-30s Celsius in some areas with increasing humidity. However, heavy rain and thunderstorms are also expected, prompting a Met Office yellow warning for rain across Wales and most of England starting Tuesday midnight. Officials warn this could cause localised flooding. Health impacts persist, with hospitals reporting admissions of severely sunburned children, including a baby. Authorities reiterated NHS advice to stay cool and urged sunscreen use. Police and fire services issued warnings following drownings and wildfires, attributing many grass fires to carelessness like discarded cigarettes. HM Coastguard reported a nearly 25% increase in call-outs. Scotland, Wales, and Northern Ireland recorded their hottest days of the year recently. Research suggests the heatwave may have contributed to hundreds of extra deaths in England and Wales. The Met Office clarified its alert system, noting the top level-four "national emergency" category, requiring government involvement, has never been used and would follow prolonged extreme heat causing widespread issues like water shortages and deaths among fit individuals.
