Researchers are uncovering hidden World War II bunkers built for British resistance fighters in case of Nazi occupation, revealing a secret network ordered by Winston Churchill. Near Selkirk in the Scottish Borders, a recently discovered operational base highlights a nationwide plan involving 500 concealed foxholes and 3,000 civilian volunteers ready for guerrilla warfare. Formed in 1940 after Dunkirk, the British Resistance Organisation recruited saboteurs from the Home Guard. Volunteers—farmers, tradesmen, and professionals by day—would vanish into underground bases without farewells when invasion alarms sounded. Their mission: disrupt Nazi supply lines via night raids using silenced weapons and explosives stored in bunkers stocked for a month. Entrances were so well camouflaged that many bases remain undiscovered 75 years later. Researcher David Blair estimates 650 Scottish volunteers among the 3,000 UK-wide, noting recruits knew capture meant execution: "They could be killed, and Nazis would hang civilians in retribution." Recent finds include a well-preserved Selkirk bunker described as "like Tutankhamun's tomb," with bunk beds and ventilation. Similar discoveries elsewhere in Scotland underscore efforts by the British Resistance Archive to map sites and honor members. Blair has identified all 100 Scottish patrols after two decades of research. The archive seeks public information via coleshillhouse.com to preserve this history.
