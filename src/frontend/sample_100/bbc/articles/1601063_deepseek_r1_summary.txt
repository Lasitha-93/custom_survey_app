UK political leaders have pledged cross-party cooperation on climate change, regardless of election outcomes. In a rare joint statement, Prime Minister David Cameron (Conservative), Ed Miliband (Labour), and Nick Clegg (Liberal Democrat) declared climate change a critical threat to global security, prosperity, and poverty efforts. They committed to ending unabated coal-fired power generation—requiring carbon capture technology for any continued use—and accelerating the transition to a low-carbon economy. The pledge, brokered by Green Alliance director Matthew Spencer, aims to reassure investors and European allies ahead of December’s UN climate summit in Paris. It counters concerns that Conservative climate skepticism might align with UKIP, which advocates repealing the UK’s Climate Change Act and questions established climate science. Spencer called the pre-election unity "unusual" and vital for maintaining the UK’s leadership in global emissions negotiations. Leaders specifically vowed to pursue a binding global agreement limiting temperature rises to below 2°C, uphold carbon budgets under existing law, and boost renewable energy. While Labour and Liberal Democrats emphasized climate action as core to their beliefs, and Greenpeace hailed a "turning point," UKIP criticized the focus as overstated. Industry voices cautioned about potential energy price hikes affecting competitiveness.
