Bangladesh and India have resolved a decades-old border dispute by exchanging over 150 enclaves, formalizing a landmark agreement signed by Prime Ministers Narendra Modi and Sheikh Hasina in Dhaka. The deal addresses a colonial-era issue that left thousands stateless. Under the agreement, India transfers 51 Bangladeshi enclaves within its territory, while Bangladesh transfers 111 Indian enclaves. Residents gain citizenship rights and can choose to remain or relocate. "We have resolved a question that has lingered since independence," Modi declared, calling it a "settled boundary." The enclaves—pockets of one country inside the other—had deprived inhabitants of basic services for generations. A 1974 pact between Indira Gandhi and Sheikh Mujibur Rahman stalled after Mujib's assassination. Both leaders also inaugurated a bus service linking Dhaka with Kolkata, Agartala, Guwahati, and Shillong, and signed agreements on trade, border security, human trafficking, and water sharing. Bangladesh's Foreign Minister Abul Hassan Mahmood Ali hailed the border resolution as a "historic milestone" in bilateral relations.
